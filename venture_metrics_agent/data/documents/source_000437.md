# The R&D Venture Studio Playbook

How to unlock impact and business opportunities from your organization’s R&D by building an in-house venture studio

Written By:David Cohen-Tanugi, Gene Keselman, Andrew Inglis, Fiona Murray, Andrew Lo, Michael Cima, Michael Short, and Dennis Whyte 

 MIT Proto Ventures

Published April 30, 2025

 Introduction 
## The US is a powerhouse in research and technology, but we are falling behind in transforming our technical breakthroughs into societal and economic benefits in an increasingly globally competitive world.

![Image 7: Iceberg](https://rdventurestudio.com/_astro/section-00-iceberg.C-AzJF74_dC2xn.jpg)

Most cutting-edge technical breakthroughs happen within organizations such as research universities, national laboratories, and private corporations with large R&D groups. But while many of these organizations have patenting offices, innovation programs, internal funding mechanisms and entrepreneurs in residence, a huge number of opportunities to change the world through technological innovation aren’t being realized: **we're only seeing the tip of the iceberg.**

This isn’t anybody’s fault: the programs and systems that exist today in R&D organizations simply aren’t equipped or structured to identify and build real, impactful ventures. **We need a better model for translating cutting-edge research efforts into sustainable products and businesses.**

**This playbook outlines a new way to fix your R&D translation pipeline and to turn innovation into a pillar of your organization.** It builds upon our experience leading [MIT Proto Ventures](https://protoventures.mit.edu/), the groundbreaking in-house venture studio inside the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), as well as from lessons learned from hundreds of engagements and conversations with leading global organizations.

### What this playbook is:

*   **A resource** for innovation & entrepreneurship leaders at research universities, national laboratories, and private corporations with large R&D groups engaged in ‘deep tech’
*   **A clear** presentation of R&D venture studios and why they work
*   **A guide** to creating a successful R&D venture studio within your organization

### What this playbook is NOT:

*   **Proposing to replace** your existing entrepreneurship center or innovation program. These entities likely play a crucial role in your existing ecosystem, assuming they are well-run and structured for success. An R&D Venture Studio complements and amplifies what your existing tech transfer programs are already doing.
*   **A guide for private investors** who want to collaborate with R&D organizations, or for R&D organizations that want to partner with private venture studios. If this is you, you may want to learn from the examples of entities like [HighTechXL](https://hightechxl.com/), [Marble](https://marble.studio/), or [Oxford Science Enterprises](https://www.oxfordscienceenterprises.com/), [High Alpha Innovation](https://www.alloypartners.com/) instead.

Section 1
## What is an R&D venture studio?

An R&D venture studio is a program that creates new products and businesses within a larger organization, leveraging R&D produced inside that organization.

 Section 1 | What is an R&D venture studio? 

## Here’s a (non-exhaustive) list of what an R&D venture studio does:

*   **Launches new products and businesses** that address a validated customer need
*   Leverages differentiated, defensible **technical capabilities** from an organization’s R&D labs
*   **Creates or improves the culture of [technology translation](https://cleanenergyfusionatmitprotoventures.netlify.app/6.-other/translational-research)** within an R&D organization.
*   **Enhances researchers' ability to bridge the gap** between discovery and real-world application
*   **Creates new connections** between disparate research groups, and between the business community (e.g. the business school) and the R&D community (e.g. science & engineering departments)
*   **Improves the quality of new venture initiatives** projects being incubated within the organization

## R&D venture studios achieve all of this by:

*   **Embedding individuals** with entrepreneurial experience (but also a strong technical background) inside laboratories
*   **Iteratively creating and refining venture ideas** that connect the dots between new capabilities in the lab (discovered through tech scouting) and large market opportunities (discovered through customer discovery)
*   **Partnering with principal investigators and researchers** to lead in-depth market discovery, validating the commercial potential of lab inventions and identifying the most impactful applications
*   **Cultivating an ecosystem** of entrepreneurs, investors, corporate ‘design partners’, and advisors
*   **Managing a portfolio of venture explorations** and working with founding teams to get the most promising projects off the ground
*   **Having an explicit structure and process** aligned with the organization’s challenges and advantages in tech translation, and prescribing criteria for companies to graduate from the studio

### In our work at MIT, we have seen countless entrepreneurial teams with a technology looking for a problem to solve (too much tech push, not enough market pull), and countless others with the opposite problem: a crucial market need identified but no good solution to offer, except perhaps the development of yet another AI chatbot.

Designed properly, _**an R&D venture studio will transform the translational pipeline**_ from labs, adding deliberateness and structure to what is conventionally a serendipitous and fumbling process, and _**delivering new ventures that possess both strong tech push and strong market pull.**_

> ![Image 8](https://rdventurestudio.com/_astro/quote-icon.DO3sSYSA.svg)Over the years of building companies, I’ve encountered many variations of the venture-building approach. One of the biggest challenges I’ve observed is that traditional venture-building models often feel like consulting services creating businesses without any real IP or technical defensibility. Additionally, studios often take an outsized equity stake without delivering commensurate value. What I admire about Proto Ventures is how it turns the model on its head by integrating the Venture Builder with the MIT research community. It’s a truly unique approach that resonates with the type of impactful innovation I’m passionate about.

 — Chris Gilbert, Entrepreneur in Residence, Mach49 

Section 2
## What makes R&D venture studios so effective

## R&D venture studios unleash the whole iceberg

### When it comes to impact opportunities from your organization’s R&D, you’re likely only seeing the tip of the iceberg.

Your institution has intellectual property (IP), licensing successes, startups that launch every year, and several 'star' entrepreneurial researchers who have launched multiple spinoff companies from their lab. But that's only the tip of the iceberg when it comes to opportunities for impact.

_**Many of us are relying on serendipity and letting 90% of the opportunities to change the world slip through the cracks every year.**_

Often, this is because promising technologies stay on the lab room floor.

*   a mere [4.2%](https://library.iated.org/view/RANTANEN2024BOT)

**of patents filed or issued by academic institutions are ever commercialized**

> **Note:** This 4.2% statistic doesn’t even take into account technical breakthroughs that were never patented because the researchers didn’t bother to disclose the invention. An R&D venture studio embeds in labs to identify the most promising inventions that would otherwise go unleveraged.

There are many reasons for this, ranging from **incentive misalignment** (researchers get promoted based on publications and academic impact, not on finding commercial licensors for their inventions), to **business misalignment** (R&D breakthroughs are often too disruptive for an existing group to commercialize without cannibalizing its existing business), to **lack of industry awareness** (researchers can’t see the potential commercial impact of what they’ve invented or don't know where to begin).

Another common reason why **90% of the iceberg slips through the cracks is because the ‘tech push’ approach** to venture building leads to overly narrow ventures. You have probably noticed that most ventures coming out of R&D organizations typically start with a transformative new lab invention and then start looking for the best commercial application for this invention: this is what is often referred to as a tech push venture.

_**The reality is that many successful businesses are built not around a single invention, but around the integration of disparate IP and innovative ideas that come together to enable a new impactful product, service, or business model.**_

By treating a single invention as the core DNA for their exploration, these ventures miss opportunities to create a broader and ultimately more successful business.

![Image 9: Cross-pollination diagram](https://rdventurestudio.com/_astro/section-02-cross-pollination-diagram.DDjyGOkV_2u9bBu.svg)

**R&D venture studios address this limitation by moving past the conventional ‘tech push’ to lab translation.** Instead, they compile a broad list of promising technologies and cross-pollinating it with a list of validated customer needs. This results in products and businesses that are well-balanced when it comes to tech push and market pull; this unique **balance is what enables R&D venture studios to formulate venture ideas that are more creative and effective than pure ‘tech push’ projects.**

## R&D venture studios leverage research groups that aren’t actively engaged in commercialization

You probably have some star inventors with a strong record of patenting and commercialization, but the vast majority of your researchers are theoretically interested in commercialization but not actively doing anything about it.

When we surveyed researchers at the MIT Plasma Science and Fusion Center (PSFC), we learned that:

*   75%

**expected that their research will have real-world applications**
*   81%

**were interested in seeing their research commercialized**

Yet only a fraction of them had ever filed an invention disclosure to the MIT Technology Licensing Office, and fewer still had actively participated in a commercialization effort beyond performing sponsored research for industrial corporations.

This isn’t the researchers’ fault: it is a symptom of the translational system under which they operate. **It simply isn’t not a researcher's job to explore commercial potential.** Moreover, there is no evidence that an organization’s endemic researchers or staff are inherently good at turning inventions into products or businesses: it is a very different skill set. As a result, most researchers would like to see their inventions commercialized, but they don’t do anything about it.

**An R&D venture studio addresses this quandary** by gaining the trust and partnering with these researchers while doing all the heavy lifting of a commercial exploration. **The venture studio becomes a translational extension of the lab**, allowing the PI to engage in research translation while focusing on what he or she is best at and most incentivized to do: new research.

![Image 10: Cross-pollination diagram](https://rdventurestudio.com/_astro/section-02-quandary-diagram.CtML-xkY_Dzbmj.svg)

## R&D venture studios ask: “what’s missing?”

### Your organization probably has plenty of resources to support innovative projects, but there is a good chance that it isn’t anybody’s full-time job to look everywhere and anywhere for latent projects that don’t even exist yet.

Traditional technology transfer offices and entrepreneurship programs often focus on supporting projects that have already surfaced, such as those seeking funding, space, or mentorship. This approach, while valuable, primarily concentrates on commercializing discrete opportunities. It rarely incorporates dedicated staff tasked with strategically planning how to best leverage the organization's complete portfolio of intellectual property. This means that **numerous opportunities to change the world through technology are overlooked.**

Moreover, existing programs are not specifically tasked with—or equipped to—**ask the critical question:** "What's missing? What unmet needs could we address? What innovative solutions ought to exist that don't yet?"

By adopting this perspective — by focusing on project inception and not just incubation or acceleration — **R&D venture studios complement existing programs** by identifying these missed opportunities to change the world, and then building startups from them. **They actively seek out the white spaces within the ecosystem.**

## R&D venture studios are proactive in shaping projects, not just mentoring them

### Most entrepreneurship and innovation programs provide mentorship to entrepreneurial teams. This is a crucial contribution, since technology commercialization and startup building are complex, nebulous journeys that greatly benefit from the advice of seasoned advisors and mentors to navigate the path.

However, **mentorship has its limits**. Good mentors leave the actual work — and the complex decisions — up to the entrepreneurial team: they focus instead on providing feedback, pointing out blind spots, and providing a cognitive framework for the entrepreneurial team to lead their exploration. In many instances, the team leads often need significantly more thought partnership and into-the-weeds collaboration with a trusted expert than what most mentorship programs offer.

**R&D venture studios** (and specifically the full-time Venture Builders that they employ) **proactively lead the venture exploration process** and shape the direction of the project, in partnership with the research team and other collaborators. Collaborations with researchers and potential co-founders take the form of hands-on weekly meetings, **with the venture builder rolling up their sleeves and personally contributing to the project.**

## R&D venture studios balance tech push with market pull

### The top reasons why ventures fail are well known: an irresolvable issue with the founding team, a product nobody wanted to buy, and the valley of death from early adopters to the mass market. A venture studio internalizes these lessons and creates ventures that are 'inoculated' against these known failure modes.

In particular, **the #1 reason why startups fail is building something that nobody wants.** University ventures are especially prone to this pitfall, in which the lab technology was groundbreaking but the product didn't solve a real customer need. Meanwhile, many business schools and private-sector venture studios create startups that go after a real market need but lack a differentiated solution or any long-term defensibility, which explains why we see so many ‘marketplace platforms’ and AI wrappers.

It is gradually becoming recognized that **extensive customer discovery is a crucial part** of the tech push process. Programs like the National Science Foundation’s I-Corps coach scientific innovators through dozens of customer discovery calls to understand their potential customers’ pain points and the requirements that their solution would need to meet in order to become adopted. This emphasis on customer discovery for ‘tech push’ projects is a good start, but the fact remains that these projects are still tied to a specific technology that forms the foundation for their team. In some cases, the technology that they are attempting to commercialize is not the most effective invention to commercialize given finite resources.

_**R&D venture studios balance technology scouting, market opportunity landscaping, and ecosystem development in equal measure before formulating projects**_

To avoid this trap, **R&D venture studios balance technology scouting, market opportunity landscaping, and ecosystem development in equal measure, before formulating projects**. For example, at MIT Proto Ventures, we conducted an extensive assessment of the commercial opportunity for enabling technologies that will eventually be required for fusion energy. This report, titled Enabling Commercial Fusion, shaped our venture exploration in the fusion sector, and enabled us to focus on businesses like American Fusion Instruments that are addressing crucial needs by the fusion startups.

![Image 11: Photo of a man reading the Enabling Commercial Fusion report](https://rdventurestudio.com/_astro/section-02-a-man-reading.C4CDznFt_17jtqj.jpg)

**A venture studio asks potential customers and investors:** what would you buy today? What's becoming untenable for your business? What have you been trying to invest in but can't seem to find a qualified startup doing this?

## R&D venture studios replace serendipity with a deliberate process

### If you ask the founders of great university startups about their company’s origin story, you will find that they invariably involve a huge amount of serendipity. In other words, if the circumstances had been slightly different, the project may very well never have come together.

Commonwealth Fusion Systems, a spinoff company from the MIT Plasma Science & Fusion Center commercializing a fusion power plant, happened because a talented group of students and postdocs took Professor Dennis Whyte’s fusion design course and, upon developing an innovative new power plant approach for the course’s final project, had the motivation, personality type, and talents to turn it into a company. The company has now raised more than $2 billion to build the first ever fusion power plant.

![Image 12: Render image of the Commonwealth Fusion Systems facilities](https://rdventurestudio.com/_astro/section-02-commonwealth-fusion-systems-facilities.K3lSoM5E_Z2ttlQm.jpg)

Here at MIT, we’ve found that R&D and tech transfer activities are highly decentralized, leaving many opportunities and ideas falling through the cracks. Our in-house R&D venture studio, MIT Proto Ventures, acts as the coxswain of the ship, watching above the fray and catching opportunities in plain sight that everybody else is too busy or frazzled to notice. In other words:

_**R&D venture studios add more deliberateness and reduce the number of serendipitous miracles that need to happen for a great business to come into being.**_

They accomplish this by **employing venture builders whose full-time job is to identify promising venture opportunities**. R&D venture studios also leverage a rigorous process for forming and evaluating ideas and turning them into ventures, which further **reduces the reliance on chance**. Finally, **R&D venture studios focus extensively on ecosystem-building and on recruiting potential entrepreneurs** so that a promising venture can still get off the ground even if the research team is not entrepreneurially minded.

![Image 13: Diagram of key factors for launching a new business or product from R&D](https://rdventurestudio.com/_astro/section-02-variables-for-R_D-product-launch.BnIA_le7_ZMMWTE.svg)

As an example of the serendipitous nature of R&D tech translation, here is a non-exhaustive list of the variables needed to align well, if not perfectly, for a new business or product to launch out of an R&D organization:

*   Principal Investigator commitment
*   Core research team buy-in
*   Supportive entrepreneurship & tech-transfer ecosystem
*   Team capability & complementary skills
*   Translatable intellectual property
*   Validated (or validatable) market need
*   Early-stage funding pathways (internal or external)
*   Regulatory awareness

For all of these reasons, **R&D venture studios are uniquely equipped to connect research breakthroughs with commercialization opportunities**, acting as a tech translation engine inside your organization.

Section 3
## How to build a successful R&D venture studio

Once you’ve understood how an R&D venture studio will help your organization, it’s time to design and build your program in a way that maximizes success.

 Section 3 | How to make your R&D venture studio succeed 
## We have undergone five years of trial and error and relentless learning at MIT Proto Ventures, and we have uncovered a set of key steps to building a successful R&D venture studio.

## Secure buy-in from leadership

### Building a thriving R&D venture studio begins with securing support from the organization’s top leadership: think VP of R&D, Chief Technology Officer, Dean of Engineering, or equivalent. If you are in one of these roles yourself, you will still want to convince your peers in the leadership team of the value of building and supporting an R&D venture studio.

Each organization is different, so you will need to emphasize and align the benefits that are most strategically relevant to your organizational strategy. At MIT, we have found that the senior administration is eager not only for more spinoff companies but also for researchers to engage in technology translation and to collaborate more with each other. To set the right expectations, you can emphasize to your leadership **the outcomes the R&D venture studio will actually deliver:**

*   **Greater translational thinking** and connective tissue within the organization
*   **More real-world relevance** for R&D projects that purport to be application-oriented
*   **More impactful** new products or businesses within 5 years
*   **A culture of** translation and commercialization

**Clarify that new programs take time;** if possible, your leadership should give the venture studio five years before making a final judgement about whether the program is successful. As shorter-term indications of success, agree with your leadership on interim metrics that you will track together, such as improvements in translational thinking and among researchers and number of new partnerships and connections within your organization.

## Build your program in-house

### It’s crucial for your R&D venture studio program to be in-house.

Some R&D organizations don't have an in-house venture studio, but they tacitly allow for-profit venture partners to hang around, looking for promising research and offering to partner with the inventors on a spinoff company. Other organizations (especially universities and national laboratories) enter formal agreements with a for-profit venture studio, like the Woods Hole Oceanographic Institute with Propeller VC, or Oxford University with Oxford Science Enterprises. While these symbiotic relationships can sometimes work, in our experience, they are inherently limited in their effectiveness compared to an in-house studio.

_**​​While symbiotic relationships with external firms can sometimes work, in our experience, they are inherently limited in their effectiveness: it’s crucial for your R&D venture studio program to be in-house.**_

The reason is simple: **a third-party studio doesn’t share your organization’s incentives or culture.** Researchers naturally sense this, and they often distrust for-profit people coming from external private companies. One of us recalls his PhD advisor enthusiastically partnering with a venture partner at a prominent for-profit venture studio about a new startup, only to discover months later that they had founded a competing company without him.

Another reason why in-house studios are more effective is that **it’s essential for venture builders to embed in labs day in and day out**, and this is inherently difficult to do if the studio is a separate entity and the venture builders aren’t employed by your organization.

At MIT Proto Ventures, two of our most successful projects — Chimera Marine, a nuclear cargo ship developer, and American Fusion Instruments, a plasma diagnostics solutions provider — only came about because David had come to know the researchers through regular informal lunches in the departmental lounge, and because his office door was wide open across the hall from their own offices.

Finally, **an in-house venture studio is equipped to partner with —and leverage— all the existing resources and programs at your organization** (tech transfer office, mentorship programs, seed funding for researchers, maker spaces, etc.). This is inherently difficult to do for a private venture studio trying to “break into” the ecosystem and being seen as an outsider.

**There are many ways to structure an R&D venture studio inside your organization.** If possible, try to set it up within your R&D or innovation group (if you work in a corporation), or within your entrepreneurship or technology transfer group (if you work in a university or national laboratory). The studio will need a managing director, a set of sponsors at the top leadership of your organization, an administrative assistant, and physical space and a budget. At MIT, we have found that the cost of operating an R&D Venture Studio is roughly $500K per venture builder per year, which covers the overall operations of the program, salaries, as well as funding for prototyping and market derisking of venture ideas.

## Forge partnerships with existing innovation programs within your organization

### An R&D venture studio complements and strengthens the programs that your organization already has, such as a technology licensing office, an entrepreneurship education center, a seed fund program, and so forth.

Approach the people running these programs early on, and help them see the value that an R&D venture studio will bring them:

*   It will create new, higher-quality projects that they can support
*   It will engage a greater population of researchers who haven’t traditionally been involved with their programs

At MIT Proto Ventures, we have built strong symbiotic relationships with the Deshpande Center for Technological Innovation, which provides research grants and coaching, and with the Trust Center for MIT Entrepreneurship, which offers entrepreneurial education and runs an accelerator program for MIT spinoff companies. Hyperion Transport Systems, one of our portfolio companies revolutionizing satellite propulsion, went on to successfully apply for support from the Deshpande Center. Similarly, Vertical Horizons, a developer of advanced power supply units for data centers, was admitted into the Trust Center’s MIT delta v accelerator program.

## Define channels with well-defined scopes

### Your organization’s R&D has potential applications in countless industries, such as health, defense, information technology, or energy. To carry out effective venture building explorations, your R&D venture studio should structure its efforts into distinct tracks (at MIT Proto Ventures, we call these ‘channels’). Venture builders will work in a single track that is aligned with their subject matter expertise and interests. If you have the budget to hire multiple venture builders to team up on the same track, that is even better: most people think better when they have a thought partner.

**How should you define these channel areas?** This will depend on the funding model for your R&D venture studio. At MIT Proto Ventures, funding for venture building comes from foundations, family officers or other university support mechanisms, so the financial sponsors play an important role in defining the topics of interest. If your venture building budget is internal, it may make sense to define channel areas related to problem areas or specific industries in which you hope to launch new products or businesses.

**A proven model is** to define a channel at the intersection of a technological area and an industry area, for example:

*   The intersection of AI and healthcare
*   The intersection of biology and mining
*   The intersection of behavioral economics and personal finance
*   The intersection of nuclear science and medicine

Either way, **it’s important to define these areas with some flexibility.** For example, when MIT Proto Ventures launched a channel in partnership with the Plasma Science & Fusion Center (PSFC), we were careful to define it as “Fusion & Clean Energy” as opposed to narrowly restricting it to fusion. This is what allowed fascinating and unexpected ventures to emerge, such as Hyperion Transport Systems, which is building upon superconducting magnet advances developed for fusion applications to develop more efficient satellite propulsion systems.

**Finding a channel scope that is neither too broad, nor too narrow, is also important**. A downside of “Fusion & Clean Energy”, as one can easily imagine, is that anything related to clean energy is potentially fair game: in hindsight, the scope for this channel was too wide. There are real efficiency gains when the venture builder becomes an expert in a particular area, and that is harder to do when the field is too large. Our next channel was scoped more appropriately: “Geothermal Energy”. This allows the venture builder to explore a range of possible innovations ranging from drilling technology to downhole electronics and heat extraction, while still allowing his learnings to compound on each other over time.

## Hire dedicated and exceptional Venture Builders

### Venture Builders (VBs) are the lifeblood of an R&D venture studio. These individuals will lead your studio’s venture building process and drive the studio’s outcomes. This is the most important variable to get right when building your studio.

_**The ideal Venture Builder has a very unique DNA: ⅓ technical postdoc, ⅓ startup founder, and ⅓ venture capital investor.**_

Look for candidates who have technical expertise, entrepreneurial chops, and experience in the industry most aligned with the channel topic:

*   They need technical expertise so that they can embed in your labs and engage meaningfully with researchers and inventions.
*   They need entrepreneurial expertise so that they know what makes a successful startup and what risks to retire early on. Also, there is a ton of tactical stuff that only a past founder can know from direct experience (e.g. when to approach your technology licensing office, when to transition to full-time,or how to handle visa considerations)
*   Finally, they should have a strong interest and ideally past industry knowledge & contacts in an industry related to the channel topic.

**To succeed, your R&D venture studio needs dedicated VBs**, and they need to be full-time for at least two years. Here are some examples of individuals that you should not try to hire as VBs:

*   **Don’t hire current researchers**. Researchers lack the requisite incentives, time, breadth, and entrepreneurship and industry experience to build ventures. While it’s a good idea to teach them about customer discovery, company roadmapping, go-to-market strategy, and fundraising, and to involve them in the process, it's unreasonable to expect that they'll do a world-class job at leading these explorations. 
*   **Don’t hire current postdoctoral associates**. VBs must be experienced in network building, evangelizing, navigating uncertainty, building a process as they go along, and so forth. Postdocs rarely have this skillset.
*   **Don’t hire current entrepreneurs-in-residence (EIRs)**. Your institution may have EIR who teach entrepreneurship courses, run hackathons, and so forth. Your EIRs are already busy mentoring, teaching, incubating, and running hackathons or other innovation events. Venture building is a full-time job and can't be a side activity. 
*   **Don’t hire non-technical business people**, such as recent MBA graduates with no technical background. VBs must have strong technical chops (for example, a Ph.D. degree in science or engineering) to engage at the deepest technical levels with researchers.

**Once you’ve hired a VB, keep them on a finite employment timeframe** so that they really treat each exploration as their next gig. This is more effective than permanent roles at places like Google X, where teams rarely launch anything because it is so cushy to stay employed at the mothership indefinitely.

**Proper incentives are crucial.** It’s important to equip your VBs to become prospective founders. They should be viewing this role as a unique opportunity to build their next gig, not as a ‘job’ working for you. To this end, your process should let venture builders heavily weigh personal fit in their process. If they aren't passionate, they're not going to launch it full time (or at any rate, it won't succeed). In contrast, some private venture studios discourage the staff who came up with the startup concept from becoming part of the founding team. This approach goes against the common knowledge that the team matters most to the success of a venture.

## Create a supporting structure for your VBs

### Beyond a salary and a desk, your venture studio should offer resources to VBs to amplify their work and keep them focused on what they do best. In particular, hire support staff or partner with other teams in your organization so that your VBs can easily get help with event planning, market sizing, legal advice, intellectual property reviews, marketing and design, and so forth.

Another key way to support your VBs is to form an advisory board for each channel. This advisory group should consist of both internal leaders in your organization and external subject matter experts. **A well-crafted advisory board will extend your VB team’s industry network, complement any gaps in their technical or business knowledge, and provide guidance and encouragement as needed.**

## Prioritize data and track key metrics

### Once you have defined a channel and hired a team of VBs, it’s time to define the metrics of success for the channel. These can take the form of objectives and key results (OKRs) that your VBs will relentlessly pursue, and that the studio’s leadership and advisory group can track to gauge progress.

The specific choice of metrics will depend on the strategic objectives of your organization and of the stakeholders who are sponsoring the channel — fiscal sponsors but also labs that are hosting your VBs.

[Download reference: OKRs from our Fusion & Clean Energy channel](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1D5Igo-Kugk44Jy0sZ29WQMyZAZpvvvHRtaxJbLF3rlw/edit?usp=sharing)

The studio’s management and advisory group can use these key metrics to keep track of progress: are the VBs doing enough ecosystem building? Tech scouting? Customer discovery? This will help you keep their efforts balanced and proactively identify blind spots or bottlenecks.

> **Note:** Designed properly, your knowledge management system will make it effortless to track and regularly report these key metrics.

## Embed VBs in labs

### The next priority is to find a lab or R&D center within your organization that is eager to host them. You should conduct this search in parallel with the VB hiring process, so that everything is already set up on the VBs’ first day. In finding the right host lab, it won’t do for the VBs to be merely tolerated — they have to be actively welcomed by the lab leadership. This is a critical precondition for the system to work properly and the extra work necessary to create a good relationship with the lab should not be neglected.

Once VBs are hired, get the lab leadership to send an email to the entire lab on the VBs’ first day, introducing the VB(s) and encouraging all researchers to open their doors to them. The lab leadership should make it that this is a strategic priority, and that the lab leadership will be checking in regularly.

Crucially, you should **ensure that the VB(s) are provided with an office or desk inside the research center**. VBs should be interacting with researchers at lunch, at regular staff meetings, in the kitchenette, in addition to during seminars, thesis defenses, and other formal research events. It helps to give VBs a budget for taking researchers out to lunch, organizing social events, and other low-cost ways to get researchers to come out of their shell.

In their initial months, VBs will ask as many researchers as possible: **what have you invented lately that might be commercially relevant?** And if something looks promising, they will follow-up by asking: **would you be interested in me serving as your commercialization sidekick?**

Finally, **let VBs engage in their own research if it’s appropriate**. For example at MIT Proto Ventures, we collaborated with PSFC researchers on a peer-reviewed article on materials for fusion power plants. This lets the VBs maintain their publication track record (if that’s important to them), build deep bonds with other researchers, and deepen their technical grasp of research in the labs.

## Discover the ingredients for new ventures

### If a successful venture is like a great cooking recipe, it all starts with discovering what ingredients you have at your disposal.

Venture building involves mixing and matching three essential types of ingredients:

1.   **Technological capabilities** are new methods, materials, or data in your labs that could potentially enable a venture idea. VBs find technological capabilities by:

    *   Attending research seminars and group meetings 
    *   Meeting with researchers to learn about what they are working on 
    *   Reading academic papers and patents 
    *   Collaborating with your IP management office 
    *   Engaging in brainstorm sessions with interested researchers

Each of these capabilities must be logged in a format that can be easily shared and tracked. For each documented capability, the VB should assess:

    *   Technology Readiness Level (TRL)
    *   Intellectual property status
    *   People (e.g. researchers) involved
    *   Time and cost to commercialize
    *   Key risks
    *   Technical specifications (if applicable)
    *   Resources (people, reports, websites)

You should look for technologies that are already developed and working for their originally intended application, at least at the lab scale (TRL 3-4), not early concepts. Focus on technologies that represent a genuine breakthrough in the state of what’s possible: you want technologies that go from zero to one, not incremental improvements.

1.   **Market needs** are pressing customer pain points that are unaddressed today and that a new venture could solve. VBs identify relevant market needs in their channel area by:

    *   Speaking with company executives and operators 
    *   Following industry news and funding opportunity announcements 
    *   Speaking with investors (e.g. VCs) and research grant administrators (e.g. DARPA) 
    *   Attending industry conferences 
    *   Reading analyst reports 
    *   Primary market research

For each documented market need, your VBs should consider tracking:

    *   Relevant industry vertical(s)
    *   Severity of need
    *   Total addressable market (today, and by 2035)
    *   Solutions to this need today, and their shortcomings
    *   Emerging solutions (e.g. recent startups)
    *   Resources (people, links)

2.   **People** are the lifeblood of any new venture. This obviously includes prospective founders but also potential advisors, investors, technologists, advocates, and corporate design partners who could play an instrumental role in one of your projects. VBs build a network of relevant people and keep track of them by:

    *   Meeting as many researchers in the lab as possible 
    *   Performing market discovery 
    *   Attending conferences 
    *   Networking with investors 
    *   And so forth.

For the people that you keep track of, your VBs should log:

    *   Basic information (organization, job title, background) 
    *   Contact category (investor, serial entrepreneur, researcher, etc.) 
    *   Context (how we met) 
    *   Goals (how they are looking to engage with your potential projects)

Keep in mind that certain types of people are harder to find than others: for example, it’s easy to build a network of early-stage investors, but it’s much harder to find a technical co-founder if the original inventor of a technology is not interested in leaving the research lab.

## Build a knowledge management system

### Venture building in an R&D Venture Studio becomes all about connecting dots and sense making. To keep track of your portfolio of identified capabilities, needs, and people, you’ll need densely linked documentation.

![Image 14: Zettelkasten method 1](https://rdventurestudio.com/_astro/section-03-zettelkasten-method.-gm6_M9u_ZEekv.svg)

There are many ways to do this. Some teams use online software like [Notion](https://www.notion.so/) or [Coda](https://coda.io/). At MIT Proto Ventures, we use [Obsidian](https://obsidian.md/), a free and flexible note-taking tool that lets us link notes to each other, easily find backlinks (i.e. past notes that link to the current note), and create connections between ideas. We use a modified version of the Zettelkasten method for note taking and knowledge management. In a nutshell:

*   We use tags for different note types: #needs, #capabilities, #venture_ideas, #people…
*   We also use tags for relevant industries and fields (e.g. #industries/mining)
*   We write each new idea in a separate note
*   We use templates to give a common format to our logged capabilities, needs, people, meeting notes, and so forth.
*   We publish our universe of notes for leadership, advisors, and other key stakeholders to follow our venture explorations in real-time

## Generate many startup ideas

### As the database of potentially relevant capabilities, needs, and people comes together, your VBs can start forming venture ideas. An idea starts by matching up a market need with one or more technological capabilities that could address it. Thus, the idea generation process will draw heavily from your databases of capabilities, needs, and people.

Like in any brainstorming exercise, **you should aim for quantity (not quality) at first**. Later on, you will eliminate many of these ideas, since many technologies don’t have the right TRL, many market opportunities aren’t aligned with your organization’s strategy, and many people won’t be the right fit for a new venture. But at this stage, **you are generating as many venture ideas as possible.**

Each venture idea should keep track of:

*   Description of idea
*   Market needs this venture would address
*   Technical capabilities this venture might leverage
*   Business model
*   Competition
*   Team
*   Outstanding risks
*   References

A key activity to generate promising ideas is to **organize guided brainstorms and product ideation sessions** with interested researchers and other stakeholders from business units, advisory groups, etc.

## Engage other potential founders early on

### There are more founder roles than there are VBs, so your studio will need to engage potential future founders early on. These individuals should not be an afterthought: they will need to build a sense of ownership in the venture idea over several months. By collaborating with your VB team, these potential cofounders will act as a force multiplier for your VBs.

At MIT Proto Ventures, we engage potential future founders by running **a fellowship program**. Each semester, we recruit 5-7 talented members of the MIT ecosystem — mid-career MBA students, PhD students, postdoctoral associates, or even alumni — who want to found a startup in our domain of interest. We **look for individuals who bring something unique to the table**, such as industry experience, startup experience, a business idea (but no technology to enable it), or a promising technology (but no clear sense of the customer need it would solve). As part of this unpaid fellowship, fellows work closely with our VBs to drive a venture exploration, and **they are explicitly equipped to become a prospective cofounder if the venture launches.**

## Select the top venture ideas to explore

### Exploring a venture idea takes time and resources: your R&D venture studio should only do this with the most promising venture ideas. We recommend a formal stage gate process to select the venture ideas to explore. Once the VBs have formed an opinion about the most promising venture ideas to explore, the stage gate should involve the relevant stakeholders (fiscal sponsor, advisors, program leadership, etc.) and should evaluate the proposed venture ideas based on the strategic metrics that are most relevant to your organization.

[Download Template: Proto Ventures Stage Gate Process](https://docs.google.com/presentation/d/1qPUGzH2SWnPkn7PwSYBsl2m_mtNBpyRAC-87RO48-uU/edit#slide=id.g2b04135c986_0_6)

At MIT Proto Ventures, we evaluated each venture idea along six metrics:

*   Severity of market need & customer willingness to pay
*   Availability of relevant MIT IP & expertise
*   Team passion level
*   Potential impact (financial and societal)
*   Crowdedness of space (i.e., are plenty of organizations already doing this?)
*   Additionality (i.e., does our venture studio add unique value, or will this idea materialize without us?)

> **Note:** Do your best to keep this selection process free of internal politics. We’ve heard horror stories of corporate innovation teams spending months on rigorous exploration only to have all of their ideas rejected, with upper leadership eventually deciding on an arbitrary concept that the venture builders hate.

## Explore your top venture ideas

Once a venture idea has been selected for further exploration, a team can form around each select idea to:

*   Refine the technological vision
*   Conduct customer research and define what the ‘product’ would be
*   Develop low-fidelity technical proofs of concept
*   Develop low-fidelity business models (e.g. business model canvas, preliminary pitch deck, and rough financials)

It’s very common for venture ideas at this stage to have either a strong market thesis and a weak sense of how to address it technologically, or vice-versa. In other words, early venture ideas are rarely balanced across market pull and technology push at the outset. To restore balance, the team will need to focus significantly on the weaker aspect. For example at MIT Proto Ventures, we commonly take a second pass at technology scouting once we have a clearer set of customer needs in mind for a particular venture idea. The market thesis for the venture acts as a "flashlight", so that our team is no longer tech scouting in the dark but instead has a mental map for what types of breakthroughs would be impactful and commercially relevant to the venture idea.

**During this phase, the VB can play one of two possible roles:** he/she can either lead the exploration as a ‘proto CEO’, or she can act as a program manager, overseeing the team’s progress but letting another team member lead the exploration. At MIT Proto Ventures, we let our Fellows run explorations, while each VB chooses a single exploration to personally lead.

During this exploration, **the team lead is responsible for:**

*   Determining what technical or business de-risking activities that need to happen at any given time
*   Documenting the process and sharing outcomes with stakeholders
*   Nurturing members of the ecosystem (e.g. business leaders, external funders, industry partners) who can support these ideas or take them forward
*   Leading the process of fleshing out, de-risking, and down-selecting the most promising ideas and iteratively refining them

## Build and launch

### The ‘Build’ phase is the logical continuation of the ‘Explore’ phase for the team projects that show the most promise. In evaluating team projects, you may find it helpful to ask each team to summarize their current state of their venture concept in a written brief.

[Download Template: Venture brief to enter the ‘Build’ phase](https://docs.google.com/document/d/172T0ljRGOJdUJPkuiJ_QR8AWxqjy89aj9hPJXLb1fPg/edit?tab=t.0#heading=h.14bfrlaenju)

Venture projects that have sufficiently validated the market opportunity, retired the greatest technological risks, and that have a strong plan to move forward are ready to enter Build mode. **This phase should focus on setting up the venture for escape velocity as a standalone company** (or as a new business line or new product in your organization).

Being ready to launch means having:

*   A core mission and vision
*   A set of co-founders and advisors
*   A technological differentiator, presumably based on patented technology from your labs
*   A vision for the first product, with visual mockups or early prototype
*   Confirmed intent to purchase from early customers, as well as market segmentation and market sizing
*   A 10-slide pitch deck that outlines the market problem, solution, business plan, competition, etc.
*   A financing strategy, with funding conversations actively under way
*   An 18-month roadmap
*   Tactical blockers identified and removed: IP options, visa considerations, company incorporation, legal representation, office space, etc.

**This phase takes roughly 6 months and requires more resources:** if your program can afford it, you should provide each team in Build mode with $50-100K for prototyping, market research, consultants, and other key activities. If other programs in your organization already provide this kind of incubation support, this is a perfect time to leverage them: your R&D venture studio provides the most value at the inception stage, and other programs may add tremendous value at the incubation and acceleration stages.

**The actual launch process will look different for each venture:** for a startup, it means the company is legally incorporated, that the founding team is in place, and that the founders are now working full-time. For a new business or product within a corporation, it means that the project has graduated out of the venture studio and has become its own business line or product development team within a profit & loss (P&L) division of the organization.

## Conclusion

### Your R&D venture studio has largely accomplished its mission once ventures launch: it has merged market insights with promising technologies from your labs to create brand new venture ideas, has turned those ideas into actionable projects, and has launched them out so that they can start solving real customer problems, generating new revenue, creating new strategic value, and making a tangible positive impact on the world.

Creating a venture is just the beginning, of course. Executing properly after the launch phase is even more crucial. Your advisory group and VB team can support its portfolio ventures by providing ongoing guidance, introductions, and other resources. If your venture studio has grant funding to offer to the companies (or you can afford to keep your founders employed by your organization even as they are ramping up their engineering and product development activities, essentially bypassing the pre-seed stage), that is even better. As this playbook has illustrated, R&D venture studios bridge a critical gap in the innovation pipeline by embedding technical entrepreneurs in labs to identify breakthrough ideas, replace serendipity with a deliberate process, and transform the most promising opportunities into successful startups.

Climate change, health crises, geopolitical turmoil: the world needs impactful innovations more than ever. At a time when the value of science, technology, and higher education are being called into question in politics and the press, R&D venture studios can help maximize the tremendous ‘return on investment’ from labs and universities, the engine of our national economic competitiveness. We hope this playbook will help act as a guide to accomplish this vision, unleashing dramatically greater innovation and impact from your organization and the world.

**—David Cohen-Tanugi, Gene Keselman, Andrew Inglis, Fiona Murray, Andrew Lo, Michael Cima, Michael Short, and Dennis Whyte**
