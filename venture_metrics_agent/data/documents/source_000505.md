## Small business innovation falls for fourth consecutive year

Small business innovation has fallen for the fourth consecutive year, and urgent action is needed to prevent the UK squandering record levels of entrepreneurial ambition, according to a major new report by the Enterprise Research Centre (ERC).

The ERC’s latest **State of Small Business Britain 2025** report brings together findings from several key business surveys. It shows that more than one‑third (36%) of working‑age adults are now starting, running, or planning to launch a business. This is the highest level recorded since the Global Entrepreneurship Monitor (GEM) survey began in 1999. Total early‑stage entrepreneurial activity has doubled since the early 2000s, stabilising at around 12%.

However, this surge in ambition is not translating into sustained growth. The report shows that the proportion of SMEs reporting product or service innovation fell from 30.4% in 2021 to just 24.1% in 2024. This marks the fourth consecutive annual decline. Export activity has also decreased steadily, falling from 19.4% of firms in 2021 to 17.2% in 2024.

The report warns that the UK’s entrepreneurial ecosystem is deteriorating. Expert ratings gathered through the GEM survey have slipped from “sufficient” to “less than sufficient” since 2020. Weaknesses in access to finance, entrepreneurial education and government policy support continue to hold businesses back. Support for female entrepreneurs also remains weaker than in France, Germany and the United States.

**Stephen Roper**, Director of the ERC and Professor of Enterprise at Warwick Business School, said:

“The UK has a remarkably resilient and creative entrepreneurial culture. More people than ever want to start and grow businesses, and we are seeing welcome shifts with more women entrepreneurs and a new generation of younger founders emerging.

“But ambition alone is not enough. We are seeing a worrying decline in innovation and exporting, the behaviours that drive productivity and growth. There is also considerable uncertainty around the future of small business support. Without urgent action to address the barriers small firms face, we risk wasting the UK’s entrepreneurial potential.”

### Key findings from the report

*   Economic uncertainty is now the most frequently cited barrier to turnover for SMEs, followed by competition and labour costs.
*   Late payments cost the UK economy an estimated £11 billion each year, with micro‑businesses (those with fewer than 10 employees) most exposed.
*   45% of businesses have adopted AI, but uptake is much lower among smaller firms.
*   Workplace mental health remains a significant issue. Presenteeism is at its highest level since before the pandemic, affecting 37% of firms in 2025. Only half of businesses have introduced mental health initiatives.
*   Micro‑businesses make up 81% of all employer firms and employ 4.2 million people. Despite this, they remain underserved and under‑researched, even though long‑term analysis shows they grow faster than larger firms.

The report also highlights a severe funding threat to the business support system. The UK Shared Prosperity Fund is due to conclude in March 2026, placing local enterprise support infrastructure at risk.

**Mark Hart**, Deputy Director of the ERC and Professor of Entrepreneurship at Warwick Business School, said:

“The Government’s small business strategy, published last summer, drew heavily on the ERC’s evidence and set out welcome commitments. However, delivery is now at risk because of funding uncertainty.

“We need a stable and coherent business support system that reflects the realities of the UK’s diverse business population. This means prioritising micro‑enterprises, tackling late payments and ensuring every business owner can access the support they need to grow.”

**Jane Galsworthy**, Chair of the ERC Steering Group, added:

“2025 was a year of mixed fortunes. While we see record entrepreneurial ambition and a more diverse founder base emerging, many firms are struggling with rising costs, tax pressures and uncertainty about future support. Urgent action is needed in 2026 to ensure the Government’s plans for growth are not undermined at delivery stage.”
