# UK R&D Spending Surges in 2025, Led by Pharma and Biotech

The United Kingdom’s research and development (R&D) investment is experiencing a notable uptick in 2025, following a period of concern over declining spend. This article analyses the trends behind the increase in – particularly the pivotal role of the pharmaceutical and biotechnology sectors – and examines how the UK’s performance stacks up internationally. It also explores recent UK policy shifts (including a tilt toward funding later-stage innovation and a hiatus in **Smart Grants**) and discusses implications for early-stage innovators. A case study on Stablepharma illustrates why strategic support is crucial to retaining domestic intellectual property, and commentary from FI Group provides guidance for UK firms navigating the current funding landscape.

## **R&D Investment Trends in the UK (2023–2025)**

After decades of striving to become a “science superpower,” the UK saw its R&D expenditure **dip in real terms in 2022 and 2023**, prompting alarm among policy-makers and businesses. New figures confirmed that total R&D spending across the UK economy fell by 2% in 2023 (after a similar drop in 2022), amounting to a **4% real-terms decline since 2021 (roughly £2.8****billion less)**. This decline was driven by reduced business and higher education R&D activity, and came **“at a time when R&D is increasingly crucial to the UK’s economic growth and industrial strategy”**. The National Centre for Universities and Business warned that this **“should be seen as an early warning sign”**, noting that preliminary OECD data ranked the UK _last_ in R&D spending growth among G7 countries between 2021 and 2023. In short, the UK entered 2024 facing a worrying R&D investment gap versus its global peers.

**Pharmaceutical and biotech companies have been a relative bright spot**, continuing to invest heavily and lead the country’s private-sector R&D. Expenditure on **pharmaceutical R&D reached £9****billion in 2023**, making it the single largest contributor – about **17% of all R&D performed by UK businesses**. According to the Office for National Statistics (ONS), **pharmaceuticals now account for roughly 18% of UK business R&D**, the highest share of any sector. This reflects the presence of R&D-intensive pharma giants like AstraZeneca and GSK alongside a vibrant ecosystem of biotech start-ups. Software and computer science, another growth area, make up about 13% of business R&D. Other industries such as automotive and aerospace also invest substantially, but the UK’s domestic R&D base is relatively _under_-weighted in areas like automotive and information technology compared to some competitors. Notably, **the health and life sciences sector’s R&D is dominated by two pharma multinationals** (AstraZeneca and GSK), which together account for an estimated 88% of R&D spending in that sector. This highlights both a strength – world-class pharma innovation – and a vulnerability if investment by those firms falters.

Despite the recent national dip, **2024 and 2025 have seen signs of an R&D rebound**. The BioIndustry Association (BIA) reported that UK biotech companies raised £1.23 billion in venture capital in just the first half of 2025, on track to **match or surpass 2024’s total**. A record-breaking £881 million was raised in Q1 2025 alone, indicating renewed investor confidence in life sciences. Major financing rounds for UK biotech (such as Isomorphic Labs’ £449 million and Verdiva Bio’s £327 million raises) and big-ticket acquisitions of UK firms by global pharma players (e.g. MSD’s £10 billion takeover of Verona Pharma) demonstrate both **high activity and high stakes**. The pharmaceutical and biotech sectors are thus **leading the recovery in R&D investment**, buoyed by strong pipelines of innovation (e.g. in vaccines, gene therapies and AI-driven drug discovery) and sustained private funding.

### **HMRC’s R&D tax credit statistics for 2023–24**

**HMRC’s R&D tax credit statistics for 2023–24** further illustrate these trends. In that tax year, UK companies claimed a provisional **£7.6****billion in R&D tax relief** on **£46.1****billion of eligible R&D expenditure**. This level of supported R&D spend was only 1% lower than the previous year, suggesting that investment has held up better than feared. However, the composition of claims shifted notably: following a reform of the reliefs in April 2023, the **SME R&D scheme became far less generous**, causing the amount of relief claimed by small and mid-sized companies to **plummet by 29%** year-on-year. The number of SME claims dropped by 31%, indicating many start-ups scaled back R&D or fell out of the incentive’s scope. By contrast, large companies under the RDEC (R&D Expenditure Credit) scheme increased their claims by 36%. As a result, for the first time ever the majority of UK R&D tax support now goes to **RDEC claimants (typically bigger firms)** – reflecting robust R&D spend by large pharmaceutical, manufacturing and technology companies. Indeed, the **“Professional, Scientific & Technical”, “Manufacturing”, and “Information & Communication” sectors together accounted for 71%** of all R&D tax relief claimed. This aligns with the prominence of pharma and biotech (spanning manufacturing and scientific services) in driving R&D activity. It also underscores a challenge: innovative SMEs in biotech and other fields have been hit by reduced tax incentives at a time when early-stage funding is already under pressure.

Nonetheless, the UK government maintains an ambitious target for R&D intensity and is banking on **life sciences to spearhead growth**. The Life Sciences Sector Plan (2025) notes that pharmaceutical R&D spending of £9 billion (2023) not only leads all sectors but is central to the UK’s goal of raising business R&D investment. The plan aspires for the UK to be the **“leading Life Sciences economy in Europe by 2030”** and among the top three globally by 2035. Achieving this will require reversing recent declines and significantly boosting both public and private R&D funding in the coming years.

## **International Context: How the UK Compares to the US, Germany and OECD Peers**

On the world stage, the UK is a substantial R&D player but **falls short of the top tier**. In 2022, UK gross expenditure on R&D (GERD) was about **£70.7****billion** (approximately **$103****billion**), equivalent to **2.77% of GDP**. This R&D intensity is _slightly above_ the OECD average (2.73% in 2022), and higher than countries like France or Canada. However, the UK significantly **lags the world’s leading innovation economies**. For example, South Korea invests roughly **4.5% of its GDP** in R&D, and countries such as **Israel, Sweden, Switzerland and Japan all exceed 3%**. The United States, with its enormous innovation ecosystem, spent roughly **$810****billion on R&D in 2022**, nearly **eight times** the UK’s outlay. Germany spent around **$158****billion** and Japan $189 billion, each well above Britain’s total. Even China – albeit not an OECD member – has rapidly scaled its R&D investment from $225 billion in 2010 to an estimated **$718****billion by 2021**, and is on track to overtake the US in absolute R&D spending.

In terms of global ranking, the **UK was fifth in the OECD for R&D spending in 2022**, behind the US, Japan, Germany and South Korea. As a share of GDP, the UK still trails the likes of **Switzerland and Korea** (spending 16% and ~100% more of their GDP on R&D, respectively, than the UK does). The UK’s relative shortfall is particularly pronounced in certain sectors. The Cambridge Industrial Innovation Policy group points out that **UK businesses are underrepresented among the world’s top R&D investors in fields like ICT (information and communications technology) and automotive** manufacturing. None of the big US or Asian tech giants are based in the UK, and in automobiles, the highest-spending UK-based firm (Aston Martin) ranks only 553rd globally. By contrast, **pharmaceuticals remain an area of UK strength internationally** – the only two UK-headquartered companies in the world’s top 100 R&D spenders are AstraZeneca (14th) and GlaxoSmithKline (33rd), both pharma leaders. This highlights the UK’s competitive edge in life sciences even as it struggles to keep up in other high-tech domains.

Policy context is also shaping the international R&D landscape. In the **United States**, the years following President Trump’s term (post-2020) have seen an intensified focus on **“reshoring” and strategic domestic investment** in science and technology. The Biden administration’s _CHIPS and Science Act_ and _Inflation Reduction Act (IRA)_, both enacted in 2022, are injecting hundreds of billions of dollars into U.S.-based R&D and manufacturing for semiconductors, clean energy, biotechnology and other key industries. These policies – essentially a form of high-tech industrial strategy – aim to **bring production and innovation back to American soil**, securing supply chains and jobs. For example, the National Biotechnology and Biomanufacturing Initiative (launched in 2022) explicitly seeks to expand U.S. biopharma manufacturing capacity and reduce dependence on overseas suppliers. The upshot is that **the US now spends over 3% of GDP on R&D** and is rapidly scaling investment in frontier areas like AI, quantum computing, and advanced pharmaceuticals. The UK, with a much smaller economy, cannot match the sheer scale of American R&D funding – but it watches these “America First” moves closely, as they influence global investment flows. UK-based companies in sectors like life sciences or microelectronics may find richer funding incentives in the US, potentially drawing activity away unless the UK remains attractive in other ways.

Germany, Europe’s largest R&D spender, offers a different comparison. Germany consistently invests around **3% of GDP in R&D**, aligning with its long-standing target (and in absolute terms spends roughly 50% more than the UK). A hallmark of the German model is **strong direct support for industrial R&D** – via networks of research institutes (like the Fraunhofer Institutes) and mission-oriented programs – alongside a relatively recent, modest R&D tax credit (introduced in 2020). Germany’s policy mix heavily features **late-stage collaborative research** between industry and academia, especially in engineering and manufacturing. This has helped German firms remain globally competitive in automobiles, machinery, chemicals and beyond. By contrast, the UK historically relied more on **indirect incentives (tax relief)** than direct funding, although that balance may be shifting now. Importantly, Germany has also ramped up strategic initiatives, for example investing in semiconductor fabrication, hydrogen technology and other areas as part of EU-wide efforts to bolster technological sovereignty.

Other OECD countries provide additional context. **France** spends less on R&D than the UK (about 2.2% of GDP), but has a very generous R&D tax credit (the Crédit d’Impôt Recherche) and strong public research institutions, which help its firms in certain sectors. Smaller economies like **Israel and South Korea far outstrip the UK in R&D intensity**, at ~5% and ~4.5% of GDP respectively (thanks to high-tech industries and supportive policies). In sum, the UK sits in the upper-middle tier of R&D-driven economies – **ahead of many peers but clearly behind the global leaders** in both investment level and growth trajectory.

The urgency of catching up is not lost on UK industry. A 2025 pharmaceutical competitiveness report by the ABPI bluntly warned that the UK is **“losing the race for investment in R&D”**, noting that since 2018 UK pharma R&D growth averaged just 1.9% annually versus a global average of 6.6%, and in 2023 the sector’s R&D spend actually fell by nearly £100 million. Likewise, foreign direct investment (FDI) into UK life sciences has dropped sharply – the UK fell from 2nd to 7th in the world for life science FDI between 2017 and 2023. Countries like **Spain** have overtaken the UK in areas such as clinical trials, and the US is leveraging trade deals and tariffs to favour its domestic pharma (for instance, recent US policy eliminated import tariffs on UK medicines, but also pressured the UK to raise drug price reimbursement thresholds). All these factors underscore that while **UK pharma and biotech are booming domestically, the global competition for these industries is fiercer than ever**. The UK’s ability to sustain its 2025 R&D resurgence will depend on how it navigates this international arena – by fostering an attractive environment at home and deepening collaboration abroad.

## **Policy Shifts: Late-Stage Focus, Smart Grant Hiatus, and Early-Stage Implications**

The UK government has introduced a series of policy changes in recent years that reshape the innovation funding landscape. A central theme is a **shift towards supporting later-stage, higher-TRL (Technology Readiness Level) innovation** – essentially pushing promising technologies closer to market – while rethinking support for the earliest stages of R&D. This approach is motivated by the UK’s perceived “innovation gap,” where world-class research too often fails to translate into commercial success domestically. However, it comes with a trade-off: some worry that pivoting resources to scale-up and demonstration projects could leave **early-stage innovation** (TRL 1–4) with less support.

One clear example is the **hiatus in Innovate UK’s “Smart Grants”**, which have long been a flagship funding scheme for early-stage tech companies. In January 2025, Innovate UK (the nation’s innovation agency) took the unexpected step of **pausing new Smart Grant competitions** in order to “develop tailored support” for startups. It was later confirmed that **no Smart Grant rounds would run during the entire 2025/26 financial year**. This pause – described by Innovate UK as an opportunity to redesign its offer – effectively put on hold a program that had provided open, responsive grants (typically £25k to £500k) to thousands of nascent projects across all sectors. The rationale was that a more targeted approach could better align with national priorities: _“We’re taking the time to ensure any future approach reflects today’s innovation landscape and has strong strategic alignment to help drive UK economic growth,”_ Innovate UK stated. In practice, this means future funding for startups is likely to be channeled through **thematic or challenge-focused programs** rather than the broad, bottom-up Smart Grants of the past. Indeed, the government’s Industrial Strategy highlighted 8 **priority “growth sectors”** (like life sciences, artificial intelligence, clean energy, etc.), and Innovate UK is concentrating support in these areas.

**Implications for early-stage innovators** have been mixed. On one hand, more **late-stage funding has become available** in specific domains – for instance, the government launched an Innovation Accelerators pilot in three city regions and increased backing for large-scale demonstrators (e.g. a £33 million fund for late-TRL R&D projects in net-zero technologies). The Life Sciences Innovative Manufacturing Fund (LSIMF) is providing up to £520 million to attract biomanufacturing investments, reflecting the push to bolster _scale-up and production_ capacity in the UK. Additionally, the recently announced **UK–US Pharmaceuticals Accord** (Dec 2025) promises to improve the commercial environment for drug makers – by eliminating US import tariffs on UK-manufactured medicines and adjusting UK medicine pricing and rebate policies to be more industry-friendly. These measures are intended to incentivize later-stage investment: manufacturing plants, clinical trials, and product launches happening in the UK rather than elsewhere.

On the other hand, **early-stage and smaller firms have faced a gap** during the transition. The Smart Grants pause was described by industry observers as _“a shock closure”_ that contributed to a **“long hiatus in UK grant funding”** for startups in early 2025. While Innovate UK has since introduced alternative initiatives – for example, a new _Start-up Connect_ mentoring program and regional Launchpad competitions – these are not one-for-one replacements in terms of pure grant funding. Many seed-stage companies that would have sought a £250k Smart Grant to prove a concept have had to either delay projects, seek smaller innovation loans, or look to **international sources of support**. Notably, the **UK’s association to Horizon Europe** (restored in 2023) has allowed British startups to once again compete for EU grants like the EIC Accelerator, which fill a similar space to Smart Grants. This is a vital outlet (as the Stablepharma case will show below), but access is competitive and not guaranteed for most. As Innovate UK itself acknowledged, **Smart Grants were “open, responsive funding”** available to any sector – losing that, even temporarily, means some high-potential ideas might struggle to find funding if they don’t align with the government’s current focus areas.

Another major policy shift has been the **reform of R&D tax incentives**. From April 2023, the UK government began overhauling its dual-scheme system (one for SMEs, one for large companies) into a **single R&D tax credit scheme**. In the interim, it introduced an **“R&D Intensive” enhanced credit** for loss-making small firms, intended to soften the blow for R&D-focused startups after the standard SME credit was slashed. Companies meeting a threshold (40% of total expenditures on R&D) can claim a higher payable credit rate (14.5%, equivalent to ~27p per £1 of R&D) under this new **Enhanced R&D Intensive Support (ERIS)**. This was a recognition that _blanket cuts_ to SME R&D relief could have unintended consequences for exactly the kind of innovative biotech and deep-tech startups the UK wants to scale. However, awareness and uptake of the intensive relief has been limited so far, and many SMEs fell just below the qualifying threshold. The overall effect in 2024 was a net reduction in SME R&D incentive generosity, contributing to the drop in claims noted earlier. Going forward, the government plans to fully merge the schemes and potentially **reintroduce more generous R&D tax credit rates** if fiscal conditions allow, aiming to keep the UK internationally competitive for investment. In late 2025, it also announced a **pilot “R&D Advance Assurance” service for SMEs** from 2026, which will let small companies get upfront HMRC approval of R&D activities and costs. This move – along with stricter compliance measures – is designed to improve confidence in the system (after past abuse of R&D credits) and encourage genuine claimants to make use of the relief without fear of inquiry.

In summary, UK policy is tilting toward an **“innovation maturity model”** – sustaining companies through later stages (with manufacturing grants, scale-up capital, procurement contracts via the NHS, etc.) so that home-grown innovations can reach the market from UK soil. This is evident in the **Life Sciences Sector Plan**, which highlights support for scaling 10–20 high-potential UK companies so they “remain headquartered in the UK”, and commitments to improve regulatory speed and market access for new medicines. At the same time, the **retrenchment of broad early-stage funding** (like Smart Grants) and the tightening of SME tax relief have raised concerns. If the front end of the pipeline is neglected, there may simply be fewer breakthroughs to feed into late-stage development down the line. The UK’s challenge will be to strike the right balance – backing winners and strategic bets, without starving the grass-roots innovation that produces the next generation of winners.

## **Case Study: Stablepharma and the Fight to Retain UK Innovation**

Stablepharma Ltd, a UK-based biotech company, offers a vivid example of both the **promise of UK innovation and the perils of insufficient domestic support**. Founded in London (with a lab facility in Bath), Stablepharma has developed a _groundbreaking “fridge-free” vaccine technology_. Its platform, StablevaX™, can transform approved vaccines and therapeutics into **thermostable products that do not require refrigeration**, addressing a critical challenge in global health logistics. In early 2025, Stablepharma reached a milestone achievement: it was awarded **€2.5****million from the European Innovation Council (EIC) Accelerator program**, marking it as one of Europe’s top breakthrough innovators.

This was a highly competitive funding round – Stablepharma was **one of only five UK companies (and 71 companies Europe-wide) to win an EIC grant out of 1,200+ proposals**. The award not only provides significant non-dilutive funding, but also validation: _“This EIC Accelerator grant is clear recognition of Stablepharma’s continued innovation… We are incredibly proud to be one of five UK companies awarded this grant,”_ said Ozgur Tuncer, the CEO. The funding is helping drive Stablepharma’s lead product – a fridge-free tetanus-diphtheria vaccine (SPVX02) – through Phase 1 clinical trials in the UK, which began in March 2025. The trial (part-funded by Innovate UK prior to the Smart Grants pause) is expected to read out by late 2025. If successful, Stablepharma’s technology could revolutionize vaccine distribution, eliminating cold-chain requirements and reducing wastage for vital vaccines worldwide.

From a UK strategic perspective, **Stablepharma’s journey illustrates the importance of accessible funding at the right time**. The company likely turned to the EIC Accelerator – an EU program – because domestic UK grants at this scale/stage were limited. The EIC award not only gives a financial boost but also opens doors to the EIC Fund (which can make follow-on equity investments). It ensures Stablepharma can advance its innovation _without_ immediately selling out or relocating. In the current climate, many British biotechs have found themselves **acquisition targets for foreign pharma giants** before they ever commercialize a product. The BIA’s 2025 report noted that **“global pharmaceutical companies continue to look to the UK’s innovative life science ecosystem to acquire future products”**, citing the multi-billion dollar takeovers of UK firms like _Verona Pharma_ and _Vicebio_ by Merck (MSD) and Sanofi. While such buyouts can validate the science, they often mean the IP (intellectual property) and long-term economic benefits migrate abroad. **Stablepharma’s success with EU funding raises a critical question**: What if the UK had its own equivalent to the EIC Accelerator, or if Innovate UK’s grant programs were capitalized to similarly back high-risk, high-reward innovators? Could more companies like Stablepharma be scaled domestically, instead of reaching a point where they must seek foreign capital or exit opportunities?

Encouragingly, the UK government has indicated it recognizes this issue. The Life Sciences Sector Plan vows to **“help high-potential UK companies to scale, attract investment, and remain headquartered in the UK”**, with a dedicated support service for scale-ups. Additionally, the new UK–US pharma deal (2025) includes measures to make the UK a more rewarding market for innovative medicines – potentially giving companies like Stablepharma a stronger business case to stay. However, the case study underlines that **grant funding and public investment play a pivotal role** at the translational research stage. Without the EIC grant, Stablepharma might have struggled to fund its clinical trial or might have been pressured to license its technology early (perhaps to a big pharma abroad). With it, the company can prove its concept on UK soil and retain control into later stages, at which point private investors or partnerships can be attracted on better terms.

In short, **Stablepharma exemplifies the kind of home-grown innovation the UK stands to lose without strategic support**. As CEO Ozgur Tuncer put it, the EIC award _“serves as a testament to the quality and calibre of the UK’s Life Sciences industry”_. The challenge for the UK will be to ensure that testament translates into tangible UK-based growth – meaning follow-on funding, manufacturing and jobs in Britain – rather than an exit that enriches others. With the right mix of policies (e.g. matching grants, co-investment funds, and fostering domestic venture capital), the UK can aim to capture the full value of breakthroughs like Stablepharma’s fridge-free vaccines, **instead of seeing them snapped up by overseas interests**.

## **Navigating the Funding Landscape: FI Group Insights and Recommendations**

For UK companies navigating this evolving R&D funding landscape, expert guidance can be invaluable. According to **funding advisers’ guidance**, firms should take a strategic, proactive approach to secure the resources needed for innovation. As recent analyses by FI Group (a consultancy specializing in R&D tax and grants) highlight, the current environment – though challenging – still offers multiple avenues of support that innovative businesses can leverage.

One key observation is that **UK R&D spending trends have set off alarm bells** about future growth, but with the right actions, companies can respond effectively. FI Group notes that in real terms, **UK R&D investment has fallen by £2.8****billion since 2021**, jeopardizing the government’s ambition to be a science and technology superpower by 2030. The causes of this decline include inflation eroding budgets, uncertainty around R&D tax incentive reforms, global competition luring projects elsewhere, and economic headwinds leading firms to trim R&D activities. In such a context, FI Group is **urging companies to act strategically to maintain momentum in innovation**. This means not simply cutting R&D when costs rise, but rather _maximising the available support_ to continue critical projects.

Concretely, FI Group’s advice for UK businesses can be summarized as follows:

*   **Leverage R&D Tax Relief**: Ensure you take full advantage of the UK’s R&D tax credits, including the new merged scheme and the **enhanced R&D Intensive credit** for eligible SMEs. Even at revised rates, these incentives can significantly reduce the net cost of innovation activities.
*   **Pursue Grants (UK and International)**: Look at grant funding opportunities such as Innovate UK competitions (e.g. Biomedical Catalyst, energy innovation programs) and international programs. Despite the pause in Smart Grants, other UKRI grants and **Horizon Europe calls (like Eurostars and EIC)** remain accessible, offering “significant non-dilutive support for R&D”. Collaborating in European projects or tapping regional funds can fill financing gaps.
*   **Balance the R&D Portfolio**: Adopt a portfolio approach, balancing long-term research with nearer-to-market development. This can make the business case for funding stronger, as a mix of projects means some can generate returns faster. It also aligns with government funders’ priorities, which often favor projects closer to commercialization (high TRLs).
*   **Prepare and Present Strong Cases**: Whether claiming tax relief or applying for grants, documentation and clarity are vital. FI Group experts emphasize _aligning projects with funder priorities and presenting compelling technical and commercial rationales_. Essentially, tailor your narrative to demonstrate how your R&D meets national innovation goals or market needs – this improves the odds of support.

Dr. Fawzi Abou-Chahine, Funding Director at FI Group UK, advises companies that **“falling R&D spending is a wake-up call”** and that they must be smart in combining different funding sources: _“By combining tax incentives with national and European grants, firms can offset inflationary pressures and maintain their innovation trajectories. The key is aligning projects with funder priorities and presenting compelling cases.”_ This approach can help firms do more with less, effectively **stretching every £1 of R&D budget by using available reliefs and subsidies**. It’s a strategy particularly relevant for SMEs and scale-ups that might otherwise be squeezed by rising costs or tightening investor belts.

Finally, companies are encouraged to **seek expert advice** to navigate the complexity. The rules around R&D tax credits have become more stringent (new compliance checks, detail required in claims, etc.), and the grant landscape is continuously evolving with new initiatives replacing old ones. Specialist advisers like FI Group (with experience securing £1.7 billion+ annually for clients worldwide) can provide clarity on what funding is out there and how to capture it. For example, they can help a company determine if it qualifies as R&D-intensive for the higher SME credit, or identify an upcoming Innovate UK call that fits the company’s technology. Many UK firms, especially those without large finance teams, are turning to such **external guidance** to ensure they don’t miss out on vital funding opportunities in this period of change.

In summary, while the UK’s R&D funding landscape in 2025 is more complex and in flux than in years past, it is still very much navigable – and even ripe with opportunity – for those who approach it strategically. The increase in UK R&D spending led by pharma and biotech shows that innovation will continue to drive growth. By staying informed on policy shifts, comparing international best practices, and leveraging all available support (from **funding advisers’ guidance** to government schemes), UK companies can not only weather the current challenges but position themselves at the forefront of the next wave of technological advancement. The collective goal of industry and government is clear: to turn the UK’s scientific potential into economic prosperity, ensuring that the R&D resurgence in 2025 is just the beginning of a long-term upward trend.
